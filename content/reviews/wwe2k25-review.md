## Finally, I Feel Like a Wrestler

Look, I'm not gonna sugarcoat it - the WWE 2K series has been on a rollercoaster. Some years it hits, some years it misses harder than a botched finisher. WWE 2K25? It's somewhere in the middle, leaning towards the good side. After sinking dozens of hours into this one, I've got thoughts.

## Gameplay: Chain Wrestling Changed Everything

Here's the thing that got me hooked - the chain wrestling. Man, this feature alone makes the game feel different from every other WWE game I've played. Instead of just mashing buttons and hoping for the best, you're actually thinking like a wrestler would. Lock up, feel out your opponent, work the limbs, build to your big moves. It's tactical in a way that wrestling games rarely are.

Previous WWE games always felt like I was controlling a character doing wrestling moves. WWE 2K25? I actually feel like I'm IN the ring making decisions. Should I go for the arm to set up my submission? Or work the legs to slow down their high-flyer offense? These choices matter now, and that's huge.

The reversal system got tweaked too, and honestly, it's way less frustrating than before. You're not getting reversed eight times in a row anymore (looking at you, 2K23). Matches flow better. You can actually string together offense without feeling like the AI is reading your inputs. Though fair warning - once you bump up the difficulty, the CPU still has some psychic powers when it comes to countering your signatures.

The addition of new grapple animations and more realistic chain wrestling transitions makes every match feel fresh. No two encounters play out exactly the same way, which is what wrestling should be about. You're not just executing a predetermined sequence of moves - you're having an actual fight.

## Graphics and Performance: My PC Loved This

I ran WWE 2K25 on my PC (RTX 4070 setup if you're curious), and let me tell you - this is the best the series has looked. The lighting is finally on point. Sweat actually looks like sweat instead of someone dumping baby oil on everyone. The crowd doesn't look like cardboard cutouts anymore. And the entrances? Chef's kiss. They nailed the presentation.

Performance-wise, I was getting a smooth 60fps at 1440p on high settings with no issues. No weird slowdowns during big multi-person matches. No crashes. No game-breaking bugs that would force me to restart. Coming from the disaster that was 2K20, this is basically a miracle.

The character models are significantly improved too. Facial animations during promos and cutscenes don't look as dead-eyed as they used to. You can actually see emotion on the wrestlers' faces now, which goes a long way in making the story modes more engaging.

Sure, there are still some clipping issues here and there - wrestlers' hair going through their shoulders, titles phasing through bodies - but these are minor annoyances in an otherwise polished package.

## MyRise: I Actually Cared About the Story

Okay, confession time - I usually skip career modes in sports games. But MyRise in 2K25? I put some serious hours into this mode, and I don't regret a single minute.

The storytelling is genuinely engaging this time around. Your choices actually matter and lead to different storylines and rivalries. I found myself invested in my created wrestler's journey from developmental talent to main eventing WrestleMania. The writing is solid - it hits that sweet spot of dramatic without being too cheesy (well, as un-cheesy as wrestling can be).

What really sold me was how the mode integrated the improved gameplay. Every match felt important. Every rivalry had stakes. When you finally get that title win after weeks of building towards it? That hits different when you've been working on perfecting your wrestler's moveset and strategy.

The progression system is rewarding without being grindy. You unlock new moves, attributes, and abilities at a good pace. Never felt like I was stuck or forced to repeat the same matches over and over just to level up.

And the branching paths? I'm definitely going back for a second playthrough to see what happens if I make different choices. That's high praise from someone who rarely touches career modes.

## Universe Mode: Superstar Edition All Day

Universe Mode is where I sink the most time in WWE games, and 2K25 gives you two flavors - Classic and Superstar. I messed around with both, but Superstar mode? That's where it's at for me.

Being able to focus on one wrestler and really dive deep into their career is exactly what I wanted. You're not trying to manage fifty different storylines at once. You pick your guy (or girl), and you're locked in on their journey. For me, it's Dirty Dom all the way. 

There's something satisfying about taking one wrestler and guiding their career from midcard to main event. Building rivalries, winning titles, creating memorable moments - it all feels more personal when you're not constantly switching perspectives.

Classic mode is still there if you want that GM-style experience where you're booking the whole show, but I found myself gravitating back to Superstar mode every time. Less overwhelming, more focused, and honestly more fun for how I like to play wrestling games.

The only downside? Sometimes the AI booking can be... questionable. You'll occasionally get some weird match-ups or title changes that make no sense. But that's been a Universe Mode thing since forever, so whatever.

## The Elephant in the Ring

Here's the reality check - as I'm writing this review, WWE 2K26 is already out with ragdoll physics and supposedly even more improvements. So the big question becomes: is 2K25 still worth it?

If you can get it on sale? Absolutely. It's a solid wrestling game that finally feels like 2K figured out what they're doing with this series again. The chain wrestling alone makes it worth experiencing if you're a wrestling fan who wants something more strategic.

But if you're deciding between 2K25 and 2K26, and money's tight? Yeah, probably just go straight to 2K26. I know I'm planning to pick it up soon because those ragdoll physics sound wild.

That said, 2K25 isn't obsolete just because the new one's out. It's still a genuinely good wrestling game with a ton of content to explore. I got my money's worth out of it, and I'm still jumping in for quick matches even now.

## Verdict

**Rating: 3/5 Stars**

WWE 2K25 is the game the series needed to be a few years ago. Solid gameplay improvements, great presentation, engaging story modes, and enough content to keep you busy for months. It's not perfect - there are still rough edges here and there - but it's a legitimate step forward for the franchise.

### The Good
- Chain wrestling mechanics add real depth to matches
- Best visuals and performance the series has seen
- MyRise mode is actually worth playing
- Superstar Universe Mode provides focused, engaging gameplay
- No major bugs or technical disasters

### The Not-So-Good
- WWE 2K26 is already out with more features
- AI booking in Universe Mode can be wonky
- Some minor clipping and animation issues remain
- Difficulty spikes can feel cheap at times

### Should You Buy It?

If you're a wrestling fan who skipped the last few entries, yes - grab it on sale. If you already have 2K24 and are happy with it, maybe wait for a deeper discount or just jump to 2K26. If you're new to the series, this is actually a great entry point to see if WWE games are your thing.

For me? I enjoyed my time with it. Chain wrestling is a game-changer, MyRise surprised me, and I got to book Dirty Dom to greatness in Universe Mode. Can't really ask for much more than that.

Now if you'll excuse me, I need to go check out those ragdoll physics in 2K26...

**Final Score: 3/5 - A solid body slam, not quite a finisher**
